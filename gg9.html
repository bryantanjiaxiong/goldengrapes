Golden Grapes

Take This From An Old Man

“Where are you going? Is this how you wish to spend the rest of your life? You can only be young for so long, you know? If you don’t do something about this soon, you’d regret it.” One of the old men preaches.

I would regret everything either way. I just need something that would bring less regrets, something that I can properly relate with. It’s an important choice to make; I need more time.

“But you’re not doing anything. You’re completely idle.” Another speaks up. “This should be your period of building up a foundation. You have to decide on a path and see where it takes you. It’d be more difficult to catch up to society the longer you remain here.”

Tell me something I don’t know. This is but a risk I have to take. There is no turning back, after all. This is a path as well.

“Why is no one else taking this risk you’re speaking of? What makes you different?” It is an elderly woman this time. “The decision you’re trying to make is the decision everyone has already made. Why can’t you do the same? No, let me rephrase that: Why do you think you cannot do the same?”

I don’t have the diligence and the discipline to persevere through just any decision. It has to be a decision that encompasses all of me. If I make a slightly wrong choice, I wouldn’t be able to give it my all – I know I wouldn’t – and then I would be lagging behind. I wouldn’t be able to compete with their personalities.

“Everyone is simply doing their best. You can think like you are thinking now only because you have the privilege of stalling. You still have resources maintaining your current lifestyle. But you should really learn to only depend on yourself.” The old woman is holding nothing back. “You’re already past the age. It’s time for you to make use of your privilege to actually decide. Not everyone has this opportunity to choose for themselves, don’t you understand?”

Exactly. I am lucky to have this privilege. So since I do, I can complain that: if I cannot be better than my competition, why try at this path at all? I would rather regret pressing my time to its limit than regret succumbing to the pressure of surviving. I have the privilege of thinking that that is not all that I want.

“Do you see your problem? You’re acting very spoilt.” The old man who spoke first returns. “Every person must grow up to be mature and realistic. You have people you should be supporting, but now they’re supporting you instead. What happens when your role is suddenly very necessary? Is this the risk you’re also willing to take then?”

I may sound like I am desperate to be right. I am, yes, I’m extremely stubborn. But if I choose a path in where I cannot properly flourish, I couldn’t give back to everyone who is supporting me now. I would not have the time to meet this ambition, this expectation, because I would be spending most of my time struggling to appeal and to simply be acceptable. Yes, it is an unreasonable and unrealistic expectation, but I don’t want to compromise and accept any less. I don’t want to accept my placement in the pyramid of society that tells me I must compromise. You don’t need to tell me to be afraid. It’s not exactly easy to be sane when I’m aware of the privileges I’m willing to risk.

“You want to live and not merely survive, is that it? And I thought today’s youth are not ones to be cheesy.” The other old man declares after a thoughtful silence. “But you’re certainly immature, though at least you’ve admitted it. It’s clear that you haven’t yet faced a truly terrible pain that might teach you to be thankful for what you already have, to be content with just any reasonably good path at all.”

Before something like that could happen to me, I have the privilege of thinking like I do, do I not?

The woman sighs deeply. “Eventually, you’re going to have to make a decision, and you must bring yourself to live with it. Perhaps that is why you’re so hesitant to make your choice. But one day, you’ll be forced to move on, whether you like it or not. And before that day comes, however much you insist that you would remain in your lane of thinking, it’s simply a fact that working on a conventional path right now is ever more advisable.”

Right. I will be regretting then. There is no other alternative; not for me. I’m sorry. It may look like your words are falling on deaf ears – and maybe they are – but though my body is idle, my mind isn’t exactly so. Maybe I would finally become practical and mature and realistic tomorrow; that might be much easier than being tormented by the reminder of my irresponsibility. But maybe not. I’m used to the latter.

A fourth person emerge out of the shadows, his presence only now acknowledged. “You certainly have the conviction to remain in pursuit of your so-called ‘one true conviction’. Is there anything else you’d like to share?” He inquires with a tone of finality.

I refuse to walk down a path to where I can’t stand out, and every conventional path is just like that. I have this persistence to remain in confusion while in search of my definition, I have the resources to maintain my persistence until my search is complete, and I have my character that I have accepted. I think it’s a rare combination in this reality of immediate competition, and I don’t wish to squander this last positivity I am holding onto. I think I should treat myself as an experiment; and if the experiment should fail, I would make sure to go off in some spectacular way. But until then…

“A clear mind yet uncleared. Nothing makes much sense nowadays anyway. Until then; until it is time.”

For glory and shame; to clarity, my every morrow.
