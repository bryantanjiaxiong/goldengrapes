Golden Grapes

A boy and his spyglass

Once upon a time, a boy was tasked to acquire golden grapes from distant lands. Like other children, this is a coming-of-age adventure meant to hone his inner-self and, most importantly, to assess if he has what it takes to seize his future. Unlike other younglings, however, they who have mastered the golden arts that allow them to multiply each grape they pluck, he did not put in sufficient effort in exploring that craft. Moreover, it is said that there are a scarce few of the highly gifted and diligent who can by themselves weave grapevines of gold, securing a reliable supply of grapes for their families after bringing back their first stash of pluckings.

Despite his clear disadvantages which are undeniably self-brought, the boy set out anyway to pursue the ending of his task. The journey was difficult but not too overwhelming, for a silver spoon could still fend off most hazards. Yet midway, he turned back home with a single black grape and a crumbled resolve, realizing that his wants and the task given to him do not align. He craves the golden basket that no one has ever found, yet accepts the inescapable fact that he is not even worthy to yearn for half a golden grape, the black grape a constant reminder of his failings, those of the past and even more in the coming future.

But there is one failure that stands as an overbearing symbol: his inability to adjust himself to general expectations. And his neighbours look to his family and whisper of his misfortune and his misdoings and his lack of decision, for what other path is there but the search for golden grapes? And his peers revise on the letters they exchanged, and he learns of their progress and he envies their great strength, then they ask him of his thoughts on his own journey and he lies that he has glimpsed a golden sliver of light that very day, and finally he retreats, because heavens forbid them knowing of his unfounded confidence in his gut, for what other path is there but the search for golden grapes? He himself does not know, yet he can imagine wondrous golden apples and golden peaches cradled in the lush embrace of the golden basket; such pictures tug at his mind like nothing else.

And with the molten remains of the silver spoon, the sap of the black grape and the smoulders of his crumbled resolve, he fashions himself a Spyglass, to look for something in the nothingness everyone else is ignoring, for the only way out of his predicament is an escape, and the only escape this act of risk. Everything he has had – every blessing he could call up – transformed into this single tool for a single purpose which might almost amount to nothing; if that is not a gamble, nothing could be one. He has decided that he could only give his all to this goal that concerns the golden basket; anything else and his will would not be at its best.

But where will that will lead him? So pray enlighten him, High Heavens above, for where else can he look to for what he wants? Is he to settle beneath his ever-piling mistakes and regrets, forever shackled by the reminders that he blundered in comparing himself to others? Their golden grapes are in hand, and they will soon return, and yet the boy idles with his Spyglass, gazing far into the horizon to see if he could find some way, any way at all, to chart a hidden path. He promises that they will soon see, that the whispers will soon be muffled praises. But he knows, without any doubt, he knows. Whenever he looks into the mirror, he recalls this that he knows: that he is, by being delusional, convincing himself that he is still searching. Yet delusional he accepts he is, yet continue striving for this delusion he definitely will, because pray enlighten him, what else can he do? Is he to- Ha! Well, is he not doing exactly that?

‘The world is pack-filled with those that have found their golden grapes, but what do the grapes avail them? Is it not naught but another insistent factor for the next generation of younglings to set out on their journeys in search of golden grapes, to be asked to invest their lives in this mundane pursuit? How new can their journeys be? No, no, that’s not it, that might have gone off on a tangent, but…Everyone has had the same goal: the golden grapes, yet out there is a golden basket, a bountiful horde of richness. Those who acquired a few will never amount to those who plucked dozens. Why are there not others who could deign to see it this way? Why do they submit to this structured natural order when it is not structured at all? There is a golden basket! Do they not see it?’

The boy mumbles to himself, but does he himself see it? He resumes his searching, peering out with his spyglass, and the wind blows and the world watches.
